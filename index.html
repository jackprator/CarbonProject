Miles Driven

Road transportation accounts for 23% of all greenhouse gas emmissions across the U.S. Miles driven per capita yearly grew 9% from 1995 to 2018. The average passenger car emits 0.78 pounds of CO₂ per mile driven.

Traveling via bicycle has shown to reduce greenhouse gas emissions, traffic congestion and health care costs.

This information comes from the University of Michigan Carbon Footprint Factsheet and the International Journal of Health Geographics

Photographer: Cayambe, CC BY-SA 4.0, via Wikimedia Commons

Vehicle Type

23.5 pounds of CO₂e are emitted per gallon of gasoline. In the U.S., gas cars churn out over 11,000 pounds of CO₂e annually, while electric cars produce nearly one-third of that amount. Electric cars have no tailpipe emissions, but emissions can be produced and measured by the source of electrical power, often through fossil fuels.

You can improve your fuel economy 7-14% by following the speed limit. Every 5 mph increase over 50 mph is equal to spending an extra $0.20-$0.40 per gallon at the pump.

This information comes from the University of Michigan Carbon Footprint Factsheet and the Alternative Fuels Data Center

Photographer: Tomasz Swatowski, CC BY-SA 4.0

Red Meat Consumption

Food accounts for 10-30% of a household's carbon footprint. Meat makes a hefty contribution to this number: 57% of greenhouse gas contributions in the average diet. Beef generates 6.6 pounds of CO₂e per serving, almost triple the amount generated by the next largest contributor, cheese.

If everyone in the U.S were to go vegetarian, we would save 330 million metric tons of greenhouse gas emissions per year. This would cut emmissions by about 5%.

This information comes from the University of Michigan Carbon Footprint Factsheet and Eshel, Stainier, and Shepon et al. Environmentally Optimal, Nutritionally Sound, Protein and Energy Conserving Plant Based Alternatives to U.S. Meat.
